Fiscal Resilience by Countries

Fiji

Binding constraints

• Buffers remain critically low relative to the scale and frequency of shocks, limiting the ability to smooth fiscal adjustment and forcing procyclical responses

• Extreme exposure to external and climate shocks amplifies fiscal volatility, particularly through the tourism channel

• Debt sustainability is fragile under stress, as weak buffers and volatile revenues increase sensitivity to shocks despite concessional financing

Fiscal context and recent trends

Fiji entered the COVID-19 pandemic with already elevated fiscal deficits, averaging around –4 to –6 percent of GDP in the pre-pandemic period, largely reflecting sustained public investment programs and infrastructure spending cycles. The pandemic led to an unprecedented contraction in economic activity, with GDP declining by over 15 percent cumulatively in 2020–2021, driven by the collapse in tourism, which accounts for roughly 35–40 percent of GDP and employment (direct and indirect). This degree of tourism dependence is among the highest in the PIC-11, where revenues are already highly procyclical and sensitive to external demand conditions. Fiji’s fiscal balance declined by around 10 percentage points of GDP during the pandemic, placing it among the most severely affected countries in the region. Across the region, fiscal balances deteriorated sharply during the pandemic, with several countries experiencing declines exceeding 10 percentage points of GDP, reflecting the scale of the shock and the limited insulation of fiscal systems from external shocks.

Fiscal balances deteriorated sharply, with the deficit widening to approximately –10 percent of GDP, and public debt increasing from about 50 percent of GDP pre-pandemic to over 80 percent at its peak. This increase exceeded the regional average rise in public debt, which went from roughly 40 percent of GDP in 2018–19 to around 65 percent in 2021, underscoring the severity of Fiji’s fiscal shock relative to peers. Although much of this borrowing was concessional, the speed and scale of the increase significantly reduced fiscal space and left Fiji’s fiscal positions highly sensitive to subsequent shocks.

The post-pandemic recovery has been strong, supported by the return of tourism flows, with arrivals reaching near pre-pandemic levels by 2023–202This has led to a rebound in revenues, particularly indirect taxes linked to consumption and tourism. However, fiscal consolidation has been partial. Debt remains elevated, buffers remain limited, and fiscal outcomes continue to be highly sensitive to external conditions.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

These results highlight a highly uneven resilience profile. Strong revenue performance in the recovery phase masks underlying structural vulnerabilities, particularly weak buffers and extremely high exposure to shocks.

Buffers

Fiji’s buffer score (0.19) reflects very limited liquid fiscal space. Cash balances that cover around 1–2 months of spending is considered adequate for shock-prone economies. As Fiji is currently experiencing negative balances, thislimits the country’s ability to smooth fiscal adjustment. Across the PIC-11, low liquidity levels are common and reflect a broader structural pattern where governments rely on continuous revenue inflows or external financing rather than accumulated buffers, limiting their ability to smooth shocks.

While the negative position of fiscal balances have improved since the pandemic (-13.7% of GDP in 2021), they remain at around –4.4% of GDP in 202 around This reflects both continued spending pressures and the structural difficulty of generating sustained surpluses in a highly volatile economic environment.

Debt sustainability

Debt indicators (0.33) suggest moderate sustainability under baseline conditions, supported by concessional financing and long maturities. However, these indicators understate vulnerability under stress scenarios.

Given weak buffers and high volatility, even moderate shocks can quickly translate into renewed borrowing needs. Debt service-to-revenue ratios remain elevated and sensitive to fluctuations in revenue, particularly those linked to tourism.

Revenue resilience

The relatively strong revenue score (0.82) reflects the cyclical rebound in tourism-driven revenues. However, this strength is not structural.

Revenue volatility remains high, as a large share of revenues is tied to consumption and tourism activity. Indirect taxes dominate, while direct taxation remains relatively underdeveloped. As a result, revenues are highly sensitive to global demand shocks and external conditions.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure performance (0.56) is mixed. While Fiji has relatively developed budget systems compared to smaller PICs, expenditure rigidity remains significant.

The public wage bill and recurrent expenditures account for a large share of spending, limiting adjustment flexibility. During periods of fiscal stress, adjustment has historically relied on compression of capital expenditure, contributing to volatility in public investment and undermining long-term growth and resilience.

Shock exposure

Fiji’s shock exposure score (0.87) perormed relatively well for the region. t enjoys the lowest import dependency of the region (63.5% of GDP)

Although the country is highly vulnerable to natural and climate-related disasters. Annual average losses from natural disasters are estimated at around 2–4 percent of GDP, with extreme events causing significantly larger impacts. This level of exposure requires substantially higher buffers and stronger fiscal frameworks than currently observed.

Policy implications

Rebuilding buffers is the most immediate priority. This requires sustained fiscal surpluses during recovery periods and the institutionalization of saving mechanisms to prevent procyclical spending during booms.

Given the scale of shock exposure, Fiji also needs to strengthen disaster risk financing, including the integration of contingent instruments (such as insurance and contingent credit lines) within a broader fiscal framework.

Debt management should remain conservative and explicitly incorporate downside risks, with debt strategies aligned to buffer accumulation and shock scenarios.

Finally, improving expenditure flexibility—particularly by protecting capital investment and strengthening public investment management—will be critical to avoid growth-damaging adjustment during future shocks.

Key transmission channels

• Tourism demand shocks (primary channel)

• Natural disasters (cyclones, flooding)

• External financing conditions

Solomon Islands

Binding constraints

• Weak fiscal buffers relative to fiscal risks, limiting the ability to absorb shocks despite strong debt indicators

• High dependence on volatile and declining logging revenues, undermining revenue sustainability

• Persistent fiscal deficits, which constrain buffer accumulation and increase vulnerability over time

Fiscal context and recent trends

Solomon Islands entered the COVID-19 pandemic with moderate but increasing fiscal pressures, driven by large infrastructure investment programs, rising public sector wage costs, and a gradual decline in logging revenues, which historically accounted for a substantial share of exports and government income. Persistent fiscal deficits have continued after the pandemic, with Solomon Islands among the countries that have not returned to balanced fiscal positions and are expected to remain in deficit over the medium term.

Prior to the pandemic, fiscal balances were already under strain, with a deficits of around –1.5 percent of GDP in 2019, reflecting both expenditure pressures and weakening revenue performance from logging. The pandemic further weakened fiscal conditions, although the direct impact was less severe than in tourism-dependent economies.

Public debt, while relatively low pre-pandemic (around 7-8 percent of GDP), has been on an upward trajectory, reaching approximately 22.3 percent of GDP in202While still moderate in level, this upward trajectory reflects persistent fiscal deficits and increasing reliance on borrowing to finance both investment and recurrent expenditures.

Post-pandemic recovery has been uneven. Growth has been constrained by declining logging activity, delays in investment projects, and continued exposure to natural disasters. Fiscal consolidation has been limited, and buffers remain weak, leaving the country vulnerable to future shocks.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

The profile indicates relatively strong performance in debt, revenue, and expenditure management, but significant weaknesses in buffers, which are critical for resilience in a shock-prone environment.

Buffers

The buffer score (0.24) reflects limited liquid fiscal space, despite relatively moderate debt levels. Cash balances are generally low relative to expenditure needs, often covering less than 1–2 months of spending, constraining the government’s ability to absorb shocks without resorting to abrupt adjustment or additional borrowing.

Persistent fiscal deficits have constrained the ability to build buffers, and reliance on continued revenue inflows—particularly from volatile sources—limits fiscal flexibility.

Debt sustainability

Debt sustainability indicators (0.85) appear relatively strong, reflecting low initial debt levels and continued access to concessional financing. Debt service remains manageable, with relatively low interest burdens.

However, this favorable position is fragile under stress. Rising deficits and limited buffers increase the risk that debt could escalate rapidly in response to shocks or continued revenue decline.

Revenue resilience

Revenue performance (0.75) is relatively strong in the FRI, but this masks significant structural vulnerabilities. A large share of revenues is derived from logging and natural resource extraction. Logging production has been on a downward trend due to resource depletion, creating a structural gap in revenue that is not yet fully offset and weakening the medium-term revenue outlook.

Logging production has been on a downward trend due to resource depletion, with output expected to fall significantly over the medium term. This creates a structural gap in revenue that is not yet fully offset by alternative sources.

Tax revenues remain narrow, with limited diversification and challenges in administration, contributing to volatility and unpredictability.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure performance (0.81) is relatively strong compared to other PICs, reflecting ongoing improvements in public financial management systems. However, rigidities remain significant, particularly in wage bills and recurrent expenditures.

Public investment remains a key driver of fiscal policy, but project execution challenges and delays reduce efficiency. During periods of fiscal stress, adjustment often occurs through delays or reductions in capital expenditure, contributing to volatility and undermining long-term growth.

Shock exposure

Strength regarding shock exposure (0.57) is moderate relative to other PICs but remains significant. Solomon Islands is highly vulnerable to:

• Natural disasters, including earthquakes, cyclones, and flooding

• Commodity price fluctuations, particularly in logging

• External demand shocks

Disaster-related losses can reach 3% of GDP, placing pressure on already limited fiscal space.

Policy implications

Strengthening fiscal buffers is critical to improving resilience. This requires sustained fiscal consolidation and improved expenditure discipline, particularly in controlling recurrent spending.

Revenue diversification is essential to reduce reliance on logging. This includes strengthening domestic tax systems, broadening the tax base, and improving compliance.

Improving public investment management will enhance the efficiency and impact of spending, while reducing volatility in capital expenditure.

Finally, fiscal frameworks should explicitly incorporate revenue volatility and downside risks, ensuring that policy decisions are robust to future shocks.

Samoa

Binding constraints

• Rigid expenditure structures limit fiscal adjustment capacity, forcing reliance on investment compression during shocks

• Buffers, while relatively strong, remain insufficient relative to repeated disaster shocks, leading to gradual erosion over time

• Revenue volatility linked to external flows, particularly tourism and remittances, affects fiscal predictability

Fiscal context and recent trends

Samoa entered the COVID-19 pandemic with relatively strong fiscal buffers and moderate debt levels, reflecting earlier fiscal consolidation efforts and prudent macro-fiscal management. Pre-pandemic fiscal balances were broadly contained, with deficits typically in the range of –1 to –3 percent of GDP, and public debt around 45–55 percent of GDP, largely on concessional terms.

The pandemic led to a sharp contraction in economic activity, driven by border closures and the collapse of tourism, which accounts for approximately 20–25 percent of GDP (direct and indirect). Fiscal balances deteriorated as the government expanded spending to support households and firms, although the impact was partially mitigated by external grants and concessional financing.

Public debt increased moderately, reaching around 60–70 percent of GDP, but remains sustainable under baseline assumptions due to concessional financing and long maturities. Low effective interest rates and extended repayment profiles have helped contain near-term debt servicing pressures. Samoa has recently improved its debt distress rating under the Debt Sustainability Framework, reflecting a combination of fiscal consolidation and recovering growth.

Post-pandemic recovery has been gradual. Revenues have improved with the reopening of borders, but fiscal space remains constrained. At the same time, repeated natural disasters—including cyclones—continue to impose fiscal pressures and require sustained public spending for reconstruction and resilience. Samoa is among the countries that have adopted fiscal rules and targets, which have helped improve fiscal discipline and reduce volatility when embedded in medium-term frameworks.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

Samoa’s profile reflects relatively strong buffers and debt indicators, but significant weaknesses in expenditure flexibility, which constrain its ability to respond effectively to shocks.

Buffers

The relatively strong buffer score (0.67) reflects higher levels of fiscal savings and access to external support compared to many PICs. Cash balances and external financing have provided some capacity to absorb shocks.

However, buffers are not fully adequate relative to the scale of disaster risks. Frequent natural disasters require repeated drawdowns, limiting the ability to sustain precautionary savings over time.

Debt sustainability

Debt indicators (0.90) are strong, reflecting high concessionality, low effective interest rates, and manageable debt service burdens. Samoa benefits from long maturities and favorable financing terms.

However, as in other PICs, debt sustainability is vulnerable to shocks, particularly given the country’s exposure to natural disasters and reliance on external financing.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.59) is moderate. Samoa’s revenue base is relatively broader than in some smaller PICs, with a mix of indirect and direct taxes.

However, revenues remain sensitive to external conditions, particularly tourism flows and remittances. Volatility in these sources can lead to significant fluctuations in fiscal outcomes. Administrative capacity constraints and a relatively small formal sector also limit revenue expansion.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure performance (0.35) is the weakest pillar, reflecting significant rigidity in spending structures.

Public expenditure is dominated by recurrent spending, including wages and essential services, which are difficult to adjust in the short term. As a result, fiscal adjustment during periods of stress often falls on capital expenditure, increasing volatility in public investment and undermining long-term growth.

Public investment management constraints, including project selection and execution challenges, further reduce the efficiency and impact of spending.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.53) reflects vulnerability to natural disasters, including cyclones, flooding, and rising sea levels. Annual disaster-related losses are estimated at around 2 percent of GDP on average, with extreme events causing significantly larger impacts.This level of exposure places sustained pressure on fiscal resources and requires continuous investment in resilience and reconstruction.

Policy implications

Improving expenditure flexibility is the central priority for enhancing resilience. This requires containing growth in recurrent spending, particularly wage bills, and strengthening public financial management systems to enable more effective adjustment.

Protecting capital investment during downturns is essential to avoid undermining long-term growth and resilience. This requires improvements in public investment management, including project selection, execution, and maintenance planning.

Maintaining and strengthening buffers remains important, particularly given the country’s exposure to natural disasters. Fiscal frameworks should explicitly incorporate disaster risks and ensure that buffer accumulation is sustained over time.

Finally, enhancing revenue stability through improved tax administration and diversification will support more predictable fiscal outcomes.

Vanuatu

Binding constraints

• Revenue volatility driven by extreme dependence on tourism, leading to large fiscal swings

• Buffers insufficient relative to the scale of shocks, limiting the ability to smooth adjustment

• Debt sustainability vulnerable to external conditions, particularly revenue recovery

Fiscal context and recent trends

Palau is one of the most tourism-dependent economies in the Pacific, with tourism accounting for approximately 40–50 percent of GDP (direct and indirect) prior to the pandemic. This high dependence on external demand made the country particularly vulnerable to COVID-19, which led to a sharp contraction in GDP of around 12 percent in 2020, one of the largest in the region. The collapse in tourism translated directly into a sharp decline in revenues and a widening of fiscal deficits. This concentration creates high revenue volatility and limits predictability for medium-term fiscal planning.

Fiscal balances deteriorated significantly during the pandemic, with deficits exceeding –13 percent of GDP, as revenues collapsed and the government implemented support measures. Public debt increased rapidly, rising from relatively low levels (around 28 percent of GDP pre-pandemic) to approximately 78% percent of GDP in recent years.

Recovery has been slower than in some peers, reflecting delayed normalization of tourism flows and continued reliance on external conditions. While revenues have begun to recover, fiscal space remains constrained, and buffers are only partially rebuilt. Palau continues to rely heavily on external grants and Compact-related transfers, which provide support but also introduce volatility and uncertainty.

Fiscal context and recent trends

Vanuatu entered the pandemic with relatively moderate public debt and a fiscal position that was manageable under baseline conditions, though already constrained by the country’s high exposure to recurrent natural disasters and a narrow domestic economic base. Prior to COVID-19, fiscal balances were generally contained, with deficits typically in the range of –1 to –3 percent of GDP, while public debt remained around 40–50 percent of GDP, largely on concessional terms.

The pandemic weakened economic activity through border closures and the collapse in tourism, though the full fiscal impact was compounded by a succession of severe natural disasters. In particular, repeated cyclones and earthquake-related shocks during and after the pandemic period imposed large reconstruction and emergency spending needs, disrupted domestic production, and weakened revenue performance. These shocks have contributed to persistent fiscal stress and limited the ability to sustain consolidation.

Public debt has risen and remains vulnerable to further shocks, while cash buffers are limited. Despite concessional financing, the country’s fiscal position remains fragile because the repeated incidence of shocks prevents the sustained rebuilding of buffers and contributes to recurring fiscal stress. In this sense, Vanuatu’s fiscal challenge is not only one of debt management, but of managing a fiscal system under almost continuous shock conditions.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

Vanuatu’s FRI profile is among the weakest in the PIC 1The results point to very weak buffers, fragile debt sustainability under stress, and limited expenditure flexibility, with resilience repeatedly undermined by the interaction between these weaknesses and high shock exposure.

Buffers

The buffer score (0.17) is among the lowest in the region, indicating very limited shock-absorption capacity. Cash balances remain low relative to expenditure needs, and the country has had little scope to rebuild precautionary savings between frequently experienced shock events.

Structural dominance of repeated shocks lead to recurring drawdowns of available resources, while subsequent recovery periods are often too short or too weak to allow for meaningful replenishment. In practice, this leaves the government with very limited room to smooth adjustment, increasing reliance on external support, borrowing, or delayed spending responses when shocks occur.

Debt sustainability

Debt sustainability (0.39) appears significantly weaker than in several peer countries. While much of Vanuatu’s borrowing remains concessional, the country’s debt position is highly sensitive to repeated shocks, which worsen fiscal balances, reduce revenue, and increase spending needs.

Standard debt metrics can therefore understate risk. Even if debt service remains manageable in the near term, the accumulation of repeated borrowing needs following disasters can quickly weaken the debt trajectory. In Vanuatu’s case, debt fragility reflects not only the stock of debt, but the frequency with which shocks force new financing needs before earlier recovery has been consolidated.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.63) is moderate relative to the country’s overall profile, but this does not imply a stable revenue system. Vanuatu’s revenues remain narrow and exposed to external conditions, including tourism flows, trade, and donor financing.

The domestic tax base is limited by the small size of the formal sector, while external shocks can quickly weaken revenue performance. Although revenue structures are somewhat more diversified than in pure sovereign-rent economies, they remain vulnerable to disruption. In practice, this means that revenue recovery often lags behind expenditure pressures after shocks, contributing to renewed deficits.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure efficiency (0.39) is also weak. Public spending in Vanuatu is shaped by the need to deliver services across a geographically dispersed island economy, which raises costs and limits economies of scale. Recurrent expenditures are difficult to compress without undermining service delivery, while reconstruction needs repeatedly increase capital spending demands.

In periods of fiscal stress, adjustment often occurs through the postponement or compression of investment, yet disaster recovery also requires sustained public investment. This creates a difficult tension: investment is both the first margin of adjustment and a central requirement for recovery and resilience. Weaknesses in project readiness, execution, and maintenance planning further reduce spending effectiveness.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.35) remains one of the defining features of Vanuatu’s fiscal profile. The country is among the most disaster-prone in the world, exposed to cyclones, earthquakes, volcanic activity, flooding, and climate-related risks.

Average annual losses from natural disasters are estimated at almost 7% of GDP, while major events can generate much larger fiscal costs. This level of exposure fundamentally changes the benchmark for fiscal policy: maintaining stability in Vanuatu requires significantly stronger buffers, more conservative fiscal frameworks, and more effective disaster risk financing than would be needed in less shock-prone economies.

Policy implications

The immediate priority is to rebuild and institutionalize buffers, recognizing that in Vanuatu buffer policy is not a secondary objective but a core condition for fiscal stability. Medium-term fiscal frameworks should include explicit buffer targets calibrated to disaster exposure and spending needs.

At the same time, disaster risk financing needs to be more systematically integrated into fiscal frameworks. Instruments such as insurance, contingent credit, and reserve arrangements should complement core buffers, not substitute for them.

Debt strategies should remain highly conservative and explicitly account for repeated disaster scenarios rather than relying on baseline assumptions.

Finally, improving expenditure flexibility and public investment management is essential. In a country where reconstruction and resilience investment are recurrent needs, stronger project appraisal, execution, and maintenance planning are critical to improving the quality and durability of spending.

Key transmission channels

• Natural disasters (primary channel)

• Tourism and external demand

• External financing and donor support

Palau

• Fragile debt dynamics under repeated shock conditions, despite concessional financing

Resilience profile (FRI results)

Palau’s profile shows moderate performance across most pillars, with no extreme weaknesses but also no strong buffers against large shocks. The main vulnerability lies in revenue dependence on tourism and external flows.

Buffers

The buffer score (0.60) reflects moderate fiscal reserves, supported by access to the Compact Trust Fund Compared to many PICs, Palau has somewhat stronger liquidity positions.

However, buffers remain insufficient relative to the scale of shocks experienced, as demonstrated during COVID-19. Rapid drawdowns during the pandemic highlight the difficulty of maintaining adequate precautionary savings in a highly volatile environment.

Debt sustainability

Debt indicators (0.73) are moderate. While debt levels increased significantly during the pandemic, and were exacerbated by access to a strong contraction in GDP, access to concessional financing wasa life line.

However, debt sustainability remains sensitive to revenue recovery and external conditions, particularly given the country’s reliance on tourism from a few key markets. In the absence of strong buffers, even moderate shocks can lead to renewed borrowing pressures.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.50) is moderate, with progress on tax reform revenue still reflects a large share of government revenues linked to tourism-related activity, including consumption taxes and fees.

Palau experiences moderate revenue volatility, as revenues fluctuate significantly with external demand conditions. The pandemic illustrated this vulnerability, with revenues collapsing alongside tourism.

While grants and external transfers provide support, they are not a stable or predictable source of revenue, and their availability can change over time.

Maintaining flexibility while protecting priority spending remains a key challenge.

In addition, the country is exposed to climate and natural disaster risks, though these are somewhat less severe than in some other Pacific economies.

The combination of external demand shocks and natural risks creates a complex vulnerability profile.

Policy implications

Reducing revenue volatility is the central priority. This requires diversifying the revenue base beyond tourism and strengthening domestic taxation systems.

At the same time, institutionalizing saving during tourism upturns is essential to build buffers and reduce procyclicality. Fiscal rules and frameworks should be designed to capture windfall revenues and prevent their use for recurrent spending.

Debt strategies should remain conservative and prioritize consolidating existing debt and borrowing for resilient infrastructure investment rather than recurrent expenditure.

Kiribati

Binding constraints

• Extremely weak revenue resilience, reflecting dependence on volatile and externally determined revenue sources

• Buffers that are significant in headline terms but insufficiently anchored in fiscal frameworks, reducing their stabilizing role

• High recurrent spending pressures, which increase vulnerability when windfall revenues weaken

Fiscal context and recent trends

Kiribati’s fiscal position differs fundamentally from that of tourism-led Pacific economies. Rather than depending primarily on domestic taxation or tourism receipts, fiscal outcomes are driven by externally determined revenues, particularly fishing license fees, grants, and returns from the sovereign wealth fund. This has historically allowed Kiribati to record sizeable fiscal surpluses in favorable years and to accumulate significant financial assets. Prior to the pandemic, this translated into a relatively strong headline fiscal position compared with many peers.

However, this apparent strength masks deep fragilities. Fiscal revenues are highly concentrated and volatile, while recurrent spending pressures remain high. During and after the pandemic, these vulnerabilities became more visible. Recent increases in recurrent spending, including subsidies and social transfers, have contributed to renewed fiscal deficits despite historically strong revenue inflows. Although Kiribati was less directly exposed to the collapse in tourism than tourism-led economies, the country faced rising fiscal pressures from increased recurrent spending, subsidies, and social transfers, at a time when revenue flows became more uncertain. In recent years, this has contributed to renewed fiscal deficits and increased reliance on withdrawals from the sovereign wealth fund.

Public debt remains relatively moderate compared with some tourism-dependent peers, but debt metrics alone understate vulnerability. In Kiribati’s case, the central issue is less debt servicing pressure than the interaction between volatile externally driven revenues, recurrent spending commitments, and weakly anchored fiscal adjustment. This creates a pattern in which strong headline balances can deteriorate rapidly when windfall revenues weaken or spending rises.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

Kiribati’s resilience profile is highly uneven. Debt sustainability and expenditure indicators appear comparatively stronger, but these are offset by very weak revenue resilience and only modest buffer strength relative to the volatility of financing conditions and recurrent spending needs.

Buffers

Kiribati’s buffer score (0.32) reflects an apparent paradox. On paper, the country benefits from the presence of a large sovereign wealth fund, which should provide substantial fiscal cushioning. Yet the FRI result shows that buffers are only moderate relative to underlying risks.

This is because the effectiveness of buffers depends not only on their size, but also on their governance, withdrawal rules, and integration into fiscal policy. In recent years, increased withdrawals to finance recurrent expenditures have weakened the stabilizing role of the fund. Market fluctuations also affect the value of fund assets, adding an additional source of volatility. As a result, buffers are not as robust as headline asset levels might suggest.

Debt sustainability

Debt sustainability (0.64) appears moderate to relatively strong. Kiribati’s debt burden remains lower than in several tourism-led economies, and debt service pressures are limited. This reflects the role of grants and asset income in financing public expenditure, as well as relatively limited reliance on borrowing.

However, debt indicators should be interpreted with caution. In a context where revenue flows are volatile and buffers can be drawn down rapidly, debt sustainability is not the central measure of resilience. Rather, it is the country’s ability to maintain stability without depleting buffers or relying on fiscal adjustment that matters most. In this sense, moderate debt does not imply strong resilience.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.24) is the weakest pillar and one of the clearest binding constraints. A large share of government revenue comes from fishing license fees, grants, and external transfers, all of which are highly volatile and largely outside domestic control.

Fishing revenues can be large, but they are inherently uncertain and influenced by external market conditions, climatic factors, and regional arrangements. Grants can also fluctuate significantly over time. At the same time, domestic tax revenues remain limited, reflecting the small size of the formal sector and limited scope for broad-based taxation.

This creates a fiscal system in which revenues can appear strong in favorable years, but are structurally unstable and difficult to forecast. As a result, medium-term fiscal planning is weakened, and fiscal balances can deteriorate rapidly when windfall revenues soften.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure efficiency (0.63) is relatively stronger than revenue resilience, but this should not be interpreted as high flexibility. Public expenditure remains heavily shaped by the structural characteristics of a small, geographically dispersed island economy, including high service delivery costs and limited economies of scale.

In addition, recurrent spending pressures are significant, including subsidies, transfers, and the costs of public administration. Where these increase during good revenue periods, the fiscal system becomes more vulnerable when revenues weaken. This is particularly problematic in Kiribati, because spending commitments can become anchored to revenue windfalls that are not durable.

As a result, even where expenditure systems function reasonably well administratively, expenditure flexibility remains constrained by the composition and persistence of spending.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.39) is stark as Kiribati remains highly exposed to climate-related risks, especially sea-level rise, coastal erosion, drought, and long-term environmental stress.

Unlike countries where exposure is dominated by large discrete disasters such as cyclones, Kiribati’s shock profile includes both gradual and acute pressures, many of which impose sustained fiscal costs over time. These factors reinforce the need for stronger buffers and more stable revenues, even if measured annual disaster losses are not always as dramatic as in some neighboring countries.

Policy implications

The central priority for Kiribati is to stabilize the use of volatile revenues and ensure that windfalls are translated into durable fiscal space rather than permanent spending commitments. This requires stronger rules to guide the saving and withdrawal of sovereign wealth fund resources, linked explicitly to medium-term fiscal objectives.

A second priority is to strengthen the role of the sovereign wealth fund as a stabilization mechanism. This means clarifying deposit and withdrawal rules, improving transparency, and integrating the fund more effectively into the fiscal framework. The objective should be to preserve the fund’s role as a buffer against shocks, rather than allowing it to become a residual source of financing for recurrent expenditure.

Over time, improving domestic revenue mobilization where feasible would help reduce dependence on externally driven flows. While the scope for large-scale tax expansion is limited, even incremental strengthening of domestic revenue systems can improve predictability and reduce vulnerability.

Finally, fiscal policy should place greater emphasis on expenditure discipline during upturns. In a system characterized by volatile windfalls, resilience depends critically on preventing temporary revenue gains from being translated into structurally higher recurrent spending.

Tonga

Binding constraints

• Buffers remain too weak relative to the frequency and scale of shocks, limiting the ability to absorb crises without destabilizing adjustment

• Shock exposure is exceptionally high, meaning that even moderate fiscal weaknesses can quickly translate into severe stress

Fiscal context and recent trends

Tonga’s fiscal position has been shaped by a combination of recurrent natural disasters, high dependence on external assistance, and a narrow domestic production base. Unlike tourism-heavy economies such as Fiji or Palau, Tonga’s fiscal system is less exposed to tourism cycles, but it remains highly vulnerable to climate shocks, remittance-related dynamics, import dependence, and aid flows. Prior to the pandemic, Tonga’s fiscal balances were broadly manageable, supported by grants and concessional financing, although fiscal space remained limited by the country’s structural exposure to shocks and the small size of its domestic economy.

The pandemic weakened growth and fiscal performance, although the effects were somewhat cushioned by large external support and continued remittance inflows. More important for Tonga’s medium-term fiscal outlook, however, has been the cumulative effect of repeated shocks—not only COVID-19, but also severe natural disasters, including cyclones and the 2022 Hunga Tonga–Hunga Ha’apai volcanic eruption and tsunami, which imposed very large economic and fiscal costs. These events disrupted output, weakened revenues, increased emergency and reconstruction spending, and placed renewed pressure on fiscal buffers.

Although public debt remains relatively manageable under baseline conditions and debt sustainability indicators have improved in some recent assessments, Tonga’s fiscal resilience remains constrained. The core challenge is not simply the level of debt, but the difficulty of rebuilding buffers and maintaining stable fiscal trajectories in an environment where shocks are frequent, severe, and often outside domestic control. In this respect, Tonga illustrates the broader point of the report: improvements in conventional debt indicators do not by themselves translate into durable resilience.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

Tonga’s FRI profile is mixed. Expenditure efficiency appears relatively strong, while revenue resilience is moderate. However, the profile is pulled down by weak buffers, only moderate debt sustainability, and a low score on shock exposure, reflecting the scale of risks relative to the country’s capacity to absorb them. The resulting pattern suggests that Tonga’s fiscal system can function reasonably well in normal periods, but remains insufficiently equipped to cope with repeated large shocks without destabilizing adjustment.

Buffers

Tonga’s buffer score (0.33) indicates that fiscal buffers remain limited relative to shock exposure and spending needs. Although external grants and concessional financing have provided important support during crises, they do not substitute for robust domestic fiscal buffers that can be deployed quickly and predictably when shocks occur.

In practice, Tonga has had limited capacity to accumulate and preserve precautionary savings between crises. Each major shock requires fiscal resources for emergency response, reconstruction, and social support, while recovery periods are often too short to allow for meaningful replenishment of fiscal space. This pattern leaves the country exposed to repeated cycles of buffer erosion and incomplete rebuilding. The weakness of buffers is therefore a core constraint on resilience.

Debt sustainability

Debt sustainability (0.53) is moderate. Tonga has benefited from concessional financing, long maturities, and relatively low debt service costs, which support manageable debt dynamics under baseline assumptions. Compared with some tourism-dependent economies, this provides an element of stability.

At the same time, debt indicators remain vulnerable to stress. In a context of repeated shocks, even moderate debt can become problematic if revenues weaken, buffers are insufficient, or new reconstruction needs generate further borrowing. The key issue is therefore not simply debt levels, but the interaction between debt, weak buffers, and high exposure to shocks. As noted elsewhere in the report, concessional debt structures can moderate immediate risks, but they cannot compensate for structural fragilities in the broader fiscal system.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.59) is moderate, but this masks important vulnerabilities. Tonga’s fiscal revenues depend on a combination of domestic taxes, grants, and external flows, while the domestic tax base remains relatively narrow due to the small size of the formal economy. Revenue performance is also indirectly affected by remittances and import-related activity, both of which are sensitive to external conditions.

Unlike sovereign-rent economies, Tonga is not heavily dependent on fishing rents or large sovereign wealth fund income streams. However, revenue predictability remains limited because a meaningful share of fiscal support comes from grants and external assistance. In addition, shocks can weaken domestic demand and imports, thereby reducing tax collection. The result is a revenue system that is more stable than in some highly concentrated cases, but still insufficiently predictable to support durable fiscal resilience without stronger buffers.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure efficiency (0.72) is relatively strong in Tonga’s profile. This suggests that, compared with several peers, expenditure systems and spending composition are more supportive of resilience. However, this should not be interpreted as implying high flexibility in practice.

As in other small island states, Tonga faces high fixed costs of service delivery, limited economies of scale, and recurrent expenditure pressures that are difficult to compress quickly without affecting essential services. At the same time, reconstruction and climate adaptation require sustained public investment. This creates a difficult fiscal environment: public investment is needed for resilience and recovery, but often becomes the margin of adjustment when fiscal stress emerges. The relatively favorable expenditure score therefore reflects comparative strength, but not the absence of rigidity.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.30) is one of the weakest pillars in Tonga’s resilience profile. This reflects the country’s very high vulnerability to natural disasters and external shocks, including cyclones, volcanic events, tsunamis, and climate-related pressures. The 2022 volcanic eruption and tsunami underscored just how severe such shocks can be, generating extraordinary humanitarian, economic, and fiscal costs.

In addition to natural disasters, Tonga remains exposed to fluctuations in remittances, import prices, and external financing conditions. Given the small scale of the economy and its geographic isolation, even shocks that appear moderate in absolute terms can have outsized fiscal consequences. This level of exposure raises the threshold for fiscal resilience: Tonga requires stronger buffers, more precautionary fiscal frameworks, and better shock-response capacity simply to maintain stability.

Policy implications

For Tonga, the most immediate priority is to rebuild and institutionalize fiscal buffers. In a country exposed to repeated severe shocks, buffers cannot be treated as residual outcomes of consolidation; they must be explicit fiscal objectives. Medium-term fiscal frameworks should therefore include clear buffer targets calibrated to disaster exposure and recurrent expenditure needs.

A second priority is to strengthen the integration of disaster risk financing into fiscal management. Tonga has already taken steps in this direction, including the use of insurance and contingent financing instruments, but these need to be embedded more systematically within the fiscal framework. The objective should be to ensure rapid access to liquidity after shocks without relying excessively on ad hoc borrowing or expenditure disruption.

Debt strategies should remain highly conservative and explicitly stress-tested against disaster scenarios. In Tonga’s case, debt sustainability analysis should be complemented by a stronger focus on the interaction between debt, buffers, and repeated shocks. This means evaluating debt not only under baseline projections, but also in terms of the country’s capacity to withstand future crises without undermining stability.

Finally, Tonga should continue to strengthen expenditure planning and public investment management so that reconstruction and adaptation spending generate higher long-term resilience returns. In a country where shocks are recurrent, the quality and continuity of investment are central to resilience.

Resilience profile (FRI results)

FSM’s resilience profile reflects very strong debt sustainability, moderate buffers and expenditure performance, and relatively lower measured shock exposure compared to some Pacific peers. However, the profile also highlights underlying vulnerabilities related to revenue structure and future fiscal sustainability.

Buffers

FSM’s buffer score (0.41) reflects moderate fiscal space, supported in part by accumulated assets in the Compact Trust Fund and relatively stable fiscal outcomes in recent years. Compared to many PICs, FSM has had greater capacity to build and maintain fiscal reserves.

However, the adequacy of these buffers must be assessed in a forward-looking context. The effectiveness of the Compact Trust Fund as a buffer depends on governance arrangements, withdrawal rules, and investment returns, all of which introduce uncertainty. In addition, buffers that appear adequate under current conditions may prove insufficient if future revenue flows decline or if shocks require sustained drawdowns. As such, FSM’s buffer position is stronger than in many peers, but still uncertain in terms of long-term resilience.

Debt sustainability

Debt sustainability (0.97) is one of FSM’s strongest pillars. Public debt levels are low, debt service burdens are minimal, and financing has been largely concessional. This provides a strong position under conventional fiscal assessments and reduces near-term financing risks.

However, as emphasized in the broader analysis, low debt does not equate to high resilience. FSM’s strong debt indicators reflect limited borrowing needs rather than a fundamentally diversified or shock-resilient fiscal system. If future revenues decline and buffers prove insufficient, fiscal pressures could emerge even without an initially high debt burden. Thus, while debt sustainability is a strength, it is not the binding constraint nor the primary determinant of resilience.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.57) is moderate, but masks structural dependence on external transfers. A large share of FSM’s fiscal revenues comes from Compact-related grants and other external sources, which are predictable in the short term but uncertain over the medium to long term.

The domestic tax base remains limited due to the small size of the formal economy, geographic dispersion, and administrative constraints. As a result, FSM has limited capacity to replace declining external revenues with domestic sources. This creates a structural vulnerability: while revenues are currently stable, they are not fully under domestic control and may not be sustainable over time.

The anticipated transition in Compact arrangements therefore represents a major risk to revenue resilience. Without adjustments, the decline in grants could lead to a widening fiscal gap, requiring either expenditure reductions, increased borrowing, or further drawdown of trust fund resources.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure efficiency (0.60) is moderate, reflecting relatively stable public financial management systems compared to some peers. However, expenditure structures remain rigid, with a large share of spending allocated to public administration, wages, and essential services.

In a system where revenues are externally driven, expenditure patterns can become aligned with current income levels rather than long-term sustainable revenue. This creates a risk that expenditure commitments become structurally higher than what can be supported in the future, particularly once external transfers decline.

Adjustment flexibility is therefore limited. Reducing recurrent expenditures can be politically and socially difficult, especially in a context where the public sector plays a central role in the economy. This underscores the importance of aligning expenditure frameworks with forward-looking revenue expectations.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.26) performs relatively weakly compared to other PICs,. FSM is still vulnerable to climate-related risks, including sea-level rise, coastal erosion, and weather-related events, but these are generally less acute in the short term than in some neighboring countries.

However, with a low level of diversification in the economy and high import dependency, the country is vulnerable to global and supply shocks.

Policy implications

FSM’s primary challenge is to manage the transition from externally financed stability to more self-sustaining fiscal frameworks. This requires a forward-looking approach that explicitly incorporates the expected evolution of Compact transfers and trust fund returns into fiscal planning.

A key priority is to strengthen the role of the Compact Trust Fund as a genuine stabilization mechanism. This includes clarifying withdrawal rules, improving governance, and ensuring that the fund is used in a way that preserves long-term fiscal sustainability rather than financing short-term expenditure pressures.

At the same time, gradual efforts to strengthen domestic revenue mobilization should be pursued, even if the scope for large gains is limited. Improving tax administration, broadening the base where feasible, and enhancing compliance can help reduce dependence on external sources over time.

Expenditure policy should also be aligned with future fiscal realities. This implies strengthening medium-term expenditure frameworks, improving prioritization, and ensuring that recurrent spending commitments are sustainable under realistic revenue scenarios.

Finally, fiscal policy should place greater emphasis on risk management and forward-looking resilience, rather than relying on currently favorable indicators. In FSM’s case, resilience depends less on current fiscal strength and more on the ability to navigate structural transitions without destabilizing adjustment.

In particular, recurrent spending commitments can become difficult to reverse once established, especially when they are financed during favorable revenue periods.

The policy challenge is therefore to maintain expenditure discipline and avoid allowing cyclical or externally driven revenues to generate permanent spending obligations that weaken resilience later on.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.49) is moderate relative to some other PICs, but this does not mean low vulnerability. Nauru remains exposed to external shocks, climate-related risks, and financing uncertainty, even if it is less visibly disaster-prone than countries such as Vanuatu or Fiji.

Because the economy is extremely small and concentrated, even modest shocks can have outsized fiscal consequences. In this sense, exposure should be understood not only in terms of physical disasters, but also in terms of economic concentration and limited adjustment margins. The relatively moderate score therefore still corresponds to meaningful structural vulnerability.

Policy implications

For Nauru, the immediate policy agenda is less about crisis repair and more about preserving current strengths and preventing future erosion of resilience. This means maintaining prudent buffer management, ensuring that fiscal savings are accumulated and protected during favorable periods, and avoiding the use of temporary revenue gains to finance structurally higher recurrent expenditure.

A second priority is to manage concentration risk. While the scope for broad economic diversification is limited, fiscal policy can still reduce vulnerability by strengthening the predictability and governance of key revenue sources and by embedding them within medium-term fiscal frameworks. The aim should be to reduce the extent to which fiscal outcomes depend on a narrow set of external factors.

Third, the strong debt position should be safeguarded by maintaining conservative borrowing policies. Nauru’s relatively favorable debt indicators provide space, but this space should be treated cautiously in a high-volatility environment. Borrowing decisions should continue to reflect downside risks and the limited room for fiscal adjustment if conditions worsen.

Finally, expenditure policy should remain disciplined and forward-looking. In a context of concentrated revenues and small economic scale, long-term resilience depends less on expanding spending during favorable years than on preserving room to respond when shocks occur.

Fiscal context and recent trends

Tuvalu’s fiscal position is shaped by an unusual combination of very limited domestic economic activity, high dependence on external income streams, and the presence of large sovereign assets relative to the size of the economy. Unlike tourism-led economies, Tuvalu does not rely on visitor receipts as a major source of fiscal revenue. Instead, fiscal outcomes are driven by fishing license revenues, grants, trust fund income, and external support, all of which can be large in relation to GDP but are also highly volatile and largely outside domestic control.

Before the pandemic, Tuvalu benefited from strong revenue inflows and sizable financial assets, allowing it to maintain relatively favorable headline fiscal positions and substantial aggregate buffers by regional standards. Public debt remained low, debt service burdens were limited, and the country’s trust fund architecture provided an important source of financing and stabilization. These features gave Tuvalu a stronger starting position than many other PICs entering the COVID-19 period.

However, this apparent strength masks important structural vulnerabilities. The country’s domestic revenue base is extremely narrow, recurrent spending pressures are significant, and the fiscal system remains highly exposed to fluctuations in externally driven revenues and fund performance. During and after the pandemic, fiscal pressures increased as spending needs remained elevated while revenue performance became more uncertain. More broadly, Tuvalu’s extreme smallness, geographic isolation, and climate vulnerability mean that even strong headline buffers can prove less robust than they appear when assessed against long-term shock exposure and financing volatility.

In practice, Tuvalu’s central fiscal challenge is not immediate debt distress, but the difficulty of translating large but volatile financial resources into durable and predictable fiscal resilience. This requires not only preserving assets, but anchoring their use within a stronger medium-term fiscal framework.

Buffers

The buffer score (0.74) reflects substantial sovereign assets and financial reserves, which provide an important capacity to absorb shocks. This is a clear strength in the fiscal system.

However, buffer effectiveness depends on how these resources are managed and deployed. In an environment of volatile revenues and recurring fiscal pressures, buffers can be drawn down quickly if not anchored in clear fiscal rules. Their role as a stabilization mechanism therefore depends critically on disciplined governance and their integration into medium-term fiscal frameworks.

Debt sustainability

Debt sustainability (0.66) is relatively favorable. Public debt is moderate, and debt service pressures are limited. This reflects both concessional financing and the presence of financial assets.

At the same time, strong debt indicators do not fully capture fiscal vulnerability. Stability depends not only on debt levels, but on the ability to manage revenue volatility and maintain buffers. If revenues weaken and buffers are depleted, fiscal pressures can emerge even in the absence of high debt.

Revenue resilience

Revenue resilience (0.22) is the weakest pillar and a key constraint. Fiscal revenues rely heavily on externally determined sources, including fishing license fees and grants. These revenues are inherently volatile and difficult to forecast.

Domestic revenue mobilization is constrained by the very small size of the formal economy, limiting the scope for a stable tax base. As a result, fiscal performance can be strong in favorable periods, but deteriorates quickly when external conditions change. This weakens fiscal planning and increases vulnerability to shocks.

Expenditure efficiency and flexibility

Expenditure efficiency (0.73) is relatively strong. However, as in other small island economies, a large share of spending is recurrent and difficult to adjust quickly.

High fixed costs of public administration and service delivery limit flexibility, while volatile revenues create pressure to adjust expenditure. In practice, this often results in challenges in maintaining stable investment levels and aligning spending with sustainable revenue paths.

Shock exposure

Shock exposure (0.30) reflects significant vulnerability to climate-related and external shocks. As a low-lying atoll country, Tuvalu faces long-term risks from sea-level rise and environmental change, as well as shorter-term fiscal pressures from external volatility.

Although not always reflected in large discrete annual losses, these structural risks impose persistent fiscal demands over time, reinforcing the need for strong buffers and cautious fiscal management.
